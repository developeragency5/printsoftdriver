Canon Device Drivers

Canon Device Drivers Made Simple to Understand

A friendly, plain-English look at how Canon's device drivers turn your everyday document into ink and paper without the jargon.

Home › Driver Guides › Canon Device Drivers

What Makes Canon Device Drivers Distinct

Canon's device drivers reflect the company's photographic heritage — colour management, paper-profile handling, and high-resolution photo output get noticeably more attention than they do in some general-purpose drivers. Even Canon's everyday office models inherit those touches.

On the technical side, Canon ships both classic Windows print-driver packages and newer host-based drivers for inkjet models, plus PostScript-capable drivers for many of the laser-class imageCLASS and imageRUNNER families. The right one is usually picked automatically by Canon's installer.

  • Strong colour-profile and ICC support for photo output
  • PIXMA, MAXIFY, and imageCLASS lines each have tailored driver bundles
  • Modern installers integrate scanning and Wi-Fi setup

The Print Pipeline From Document to Paper

When you press "Print", your document does not jump straight to the device. It takes a short journey through several layers — the application, the operating system's print spooler, and finally the Canon-specific driver that knows the exact dialect your device speaks.

Each layer adds something useful. The spooler queues your job so the app does not have to wait. The driver translates rich page elements into the device's command language. Canon's drivers, like those of every major manufacturer, also handle small but important details: paper size matching, colour profile selection, and graceful handling of two-sided output.

Common Driver Concepts You Will Meet

Across the output world, a few terms come up again and again. PCL is a page description language created originally for laser devices, optimised for speed. PostScript is a richer language used in graphics-heavy workflows. Many Canon drivers ship with both, letting your operating system pick the right one for the job.

You may also see references to a "host-based" driver. These do most of the rendering work on your computer instead of on the device itself, which keeps the device cheaper and simpler — at the cost of slightly more CPU time on your end.
