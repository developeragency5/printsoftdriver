RICOH PERIPHERAL DRIVERS

Ricoh Peripheral Drivers Made Simple to Understand

A friendly, plain-English look at how Ricoh's peripheral drivers turn your everyday document into ink and paper without the jargon.

Home › Driver Guides › Ricoh Peripheral Drivers

What Makes Ricoh Peripheral Drivers Distinct

Ricoh focuses heavily on multifunction peripherals used in busy offices, and its drivers reflect that. Expect strong scanning, finishing, and stapling support, with detailed control over paper trays, manual feed, and finishing units like hole punches and booklet makers.

Ricoh's modern drivers are split into two families: a classic per-model driver and a universal driver that works across many recent models. Both inherit Ricoh's distinctive 'one-click presets' feature, which lets users save a complete driver configuration as a single named profile.

  • Universal V4 driver targets modern Windows print stack
  • PCL6, PostScript, and Ricoh-specific RPCS variants
  • Strong finishing and tray-handling controls

The Print Pipeline From Document to Paper

When you press "Print", your document does not jump straight to the peripheral. It takes a short journey through several layers — the application, the operating system's print spooler, and finally the Ricoh-specific driver that knows the exact dialect your peripheral speaks.

Each layer adds something useful. The spooler queues your job so the app does not have to wait. The driver translates rich page elements into the peripheral's command language. Ricoh's drivers, like those of every major manufacturer, also handle small but important details: paper size matching, colour profile selection, and graceful handling of two-sided output.

Common Driver Concepts You Will Meet

Across the output world, a few terms come up again and again. PCL is a page description language created originally for laser peripherals, optimised for speed. PostScript is a richer language used in graphics-heavy workflows. Many Ricoh drivers ship with both, letting your operating system pick the right one for the job.

You may also see references to a "host-based" driver. These do most of the rendering work on your computer instead of on the peripheral itself, which keeps the peripheral cheaper and simpler — at the cost of slightly more CPU time on your end.
