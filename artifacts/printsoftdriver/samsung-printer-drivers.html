SAMSUNG PRINTER DRIVERS

Samsung Printer Drivers Made Simple to Understand

A friendly, plain-English look at how Samsung's printer drivers turn your everyday document into ink and paper without the jargon.

Home › Driver Guides › Samsung Printer Drivers

What Makes Samsung Printer Drivers Distinct

Samsung's printer business was acquired by HP a few years ago, which means today's Samsung-branded printer drivers often lead users to HP's support pages. The user experience is gradually converging with HP's universal driver model, which simplifies long-term maintenance.

Older Samsung-branded multifunction printers still have their original Easy Printer Manager and Samsung Universal Print Driver, but new drivers are now released and signed by HP. This is a smooth transition for end users — settings pages remain familiar.

  • Older Samsung Universal Print Driver still available for legacy models
  • New driver releases now flow through HP's support channels
  • Samsung firmware updates and HP-branded drivers can coexist

The Print Pipeline From Document to Paper

When you press "Print", your document does not jump straight to the printer. It takes a short journey through several layers — the application, the operating system's print spooler, and finally the Samsung-specific driver that knows the exact dialect your printer speaks.

Each layer adds something useful. The spooler queues your job so the app does not have to wait. The driver translates rich page elements into the printer's command language. Samsung's drivers, like those of every major manufacturer, also handle small but important details: paper size matching, colour profile selection, and graceful handling of two-sided printing.

Common Driver Concepts You Will Meet

Across the printing world, a few terms come up again and again. PCL is a page description language created originally for laser printers, optimised for speed. PostScript is a richer language used in graphics-heavy workflows. Many Samsung drivers ship with both, letting your operating system pick the right one for the job.

You may also see references to a "host-based" driver. These do most of the rendering work on your computer instead of on the printer itself, which keeps the printer cheaper and simpler — at the cost of slightly more CPU time on your end.
